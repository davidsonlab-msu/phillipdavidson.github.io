Welcome!

I am a Postdoctoral Fellow in Armin Moczek's lab in the Department of Biology at Indiana University.

I use genomics to study how developmental and gene regulatory mechanisms evolve to generate organismal diversification and adaptation.

Current Research

    Plasticity and evolvability of gene regulatory networks

    I am interested in the evolution and plasticity of gene networks, and their contribution to organismal diversity. Currently, I am researching beetle horns to better characterize the role of cis-regulatory elements (e.g. promoters and enhancers) in generating inter- and intraspecific variation in trait development. To do this, I am analyzing nutritional and sex-dependent changes in gene regulation within and across Onthophagine beetle species.

    Dung beetle genomes

    I am also leading a genome project focused on the assembly, annotation, and comparison of several dung beetle genomes in an effort to understand the genomic basis for adaptations and behaviors associated with their unique ecology.

Past Research

For my PhD work, I worked with Dr. Greg Wray at Duke University studying the genomic and developmental drivers of life history evolution in echinoderms. Specifically, I studied how gene regulation and physiology has evolved during the acquisition of non-feeding larval development (lecithotrophy) in Heliocidaris erythrogramma.

Much of this work is done in collaboration with Dr. Maria Byrne at the University of Sydney.

    Cis-regulatory evolution and evo-devo

    My main dissertation project investigated how developmental cis-regulatory mechanisms have evolved in H. erythrogramma relative to indirect, planktotrophic sea urchins species. Here, I identified changes in accessibility and nucleotide sequences of regulatory elements such as promoter and enhancers in H. erythrogramma near critical developmental network genes. Collectively this work demonstrates how distinct evolutionary changes in regulatory element function may underlie changes in embryonic cell fate specification in this species, and evolution of developmental mechanisms more broadly (manuscript in prep).

    Sea urchin comparative genomics

    I also assembled, annotated, and compared the genomes of a few different sea urchin species at chromosomal-resolution including Lytechinus variegatus (now published), H. erythrogramma, and H. tuberculata. The aim of this work is to identify genomic patterns underlying life history evolution in sea urchins.

    Global climate change and marine invertebrate development

    This projected included researching the effect of ocean acidification on gene expression during embryogenesis and larval development in H. erythrogramma to interpret how future global climate change may affect marine invertebrate development, settlement, and dispersal.

    Life history evolution and developmental physiology

    This work used liquid chromatography tandem-mass spectrometry to analyze lipidomic and proteomic differences in the eggs and larvae of H. erythrogramma, H. tuberculata, and Lytechinus variegatus (another planktotrophic sea urchin). The aim of this study was to better understand molecular-level, metabolic adaptations underlying rapid, non-feeding larval development.

    Ctenophore evo-devo

    I previously worked as an undergraduate in the Browne Lab at the University of Miami studying the evolution and development of ctenophores. I researched embryonic gene expression in Mnemiopsis leidyi (pictured left) using RNA-sequencing to better understand activation of the zygotic genome and early blastomere fate specification.
